We're Walking Into A Trap: Labour's Brexit Nightmare

A Labour activist's despair at the party's strategic failures ahead of what feels like an inevitable electoral disaster

I can’t sleep. It’s 2am and I’m staring at the ceiling, knowing that we’re about to walk into the biggest electoral disaster in Labour’s history. And the worst part? We’re doing it to ourselves.

The election everyone’s talking about hasn’t been called yet, but it’s coming. You can feel it in the air. Boris Johnson’s playing his game perfectly while we’re tying ourselves in knots. When it comes, we’re going to get annihilated, and I’m not sure the party will survive it.

The Brexit Mess That’s Killing Us

Our Brexit position is political suicide, and everyone knows it except the people making the decisions in Westminster. We’re trying to be all things to all people and ending up as nothing to anyone1.

In Ceredigion, I’m getting it from both sides. Remainers are furious we’re not just backing Revoke like the Lib Dems. Leavers think we’re betraying democracy with our second referendum nonsense. And I can’t blame either of them because our position makes no bloody sense.

We’re saying we’ll negotiate a new deal, then campaign against it? What kind of message is that? “Vote Labour - we’ll negotiate something we don’t believe in!” It’s insane. Meanwhile, Johnson’s got three words: “Get Brexit Done.” Simple, clear, wrong - but effective.

My comrades and I spent Sunday afternoon in Aberystwyth town centre trying to explain Labour’s Brexit position. After three hours, we’d convinced precisely nobody, including ourselves.

The Anti-Semitism Crisis We Can’t Shake

And then there’s the elephant in the room. The anti-Semitism crisis is destroying us from within. Every day brings new headlines, new allegations, new investigations. The Jewish community feels betrayed, our MPs are at war with each other, and the leadership seems paralyzed.

I’ve been a socialist all my life. Fighting racism has always been at the core of what we do. But we’ve let this poison take root in our party, and now we’re paying the price. Good comrades are leaving, Jewish members feel unsafe, and the Tories are using it to batter us daily.

The worst part is how tone-deaf the leadership response has been. When the Jewish community is telling you there’s a problem, you listen. You don’t argue, you don’t deflect, you don’t blame the media. You fix it. We haven’t fixed it.

The Liberal Democrat Opportunists

Meanwhile, the Lib Dems are having a field day. Jo Swinson’s positioning herself as the only “true” remain candidate, picking off our seats one by one2. They’re utterly shameless - the party that propped up austerity for five years is now posing as the progressive alternative.

But it’s working. In university towns, in middle-class remain areas, former Labour voters are flocking to them. Here in Wales, they’re targeting seats like Montgomeryshire and Brecon. They smell blood in the water.

The irony is painful. We’re losing remainers to a party led by someone who was in coalition with the Tories while we were voting against every cut, every attack on the poor, every assault on the welfare state.

Money Talks, And We Can’t Afford To Speak

The Tories have revolutionized campaigning, and we’re still fighting the last war. They’re flooding postal votes with glossy, personalized leaflets that must cost a fortune3. Professional photography, targeted messaging, expensive printing - it’s like they have unlimited money.

Because they do.

My constituency party can barely afford to print black and white leaflets on cheap paper. We’re doing our canvassing with homemade rosettes and clipboards held together with tape. Meanwhile, the Tories are sending out what looks like professional magazines to every household.

The contrast is stark and demoralizing. We turn up with our battered folders and worn-out shoes, talking about transforming society. They arrive with slick materials and simple messages that cut through our complex policy positions like a knife through butter.

The Ground War We’re Already Losing

I spent yesterday evening with comrades in Machynlleth, and the mood is grim. These are people who’ve been fighting for Labour for decades, who walked through hell for Jeremy in 2017. Now they’re talking about damage limitation.

“How many seats will we lose?” That’s the question everyone’s asking. Not “Can we win?” Not even “Can we deny them a majority?” Just “How bad will it be?”

The activists are exhausted. Two years of Brexit rows, endless factional fighting, and constant crisis management have taken their toll. People who were energized in 2017 are just going through the motions now. The phone banks are half-empty, the street stalls understaffed.

When Good People Give Up

Sarah, who’s run our local women’s group for fifteen years, told me last week she’s thinking of stepping back. “I can’t defend this anymore,” she said. “I can’t go to doors and explain why we’re tearing ourselves apart while people need help.”

She’s not alone. Some of our best activists are burning out, not from overwork but from despair. They joined Labour to fight for social justice, not to spend their time explaining why we can’t take a clear position on the defining issue of our time.

The Coming Storm

When the election is called - and it will be soon - we’ll face a perfect storm. A simple Tory message against our muddled position. A Liberal Democrat surge in our remain seats. A media that’s already sharpening its knives. And all of it funded by dark money we can’t hope to match.

The tragedy is that our policies are popular. Free broadband, Green New Deal, investment in public services - people want what we’re offering. But they can’t hear us over the Brexit noise and the anti-Semitism crisis.

The Establishment Wins Again

This is what the establishment wanted all along. They couldn’t beat Corbynism at the ballot box in 2017, so they’ve destroyed it from within. Brexit was the weapon, anti-Semitism was the ammunition, and our own inability to take clear positions was the target.

They’ve turned our greatest strength - our diversity of views - into our greatest weakness. We’re too democratic for our own good, too willing to debate when we should be deciding, too concerned with process when we should be fighting.

A Personal Confession

I’ve been defending Jeremy for four years. Through the leadership challenges, through the media onslaught, through every crisis and controversy. I still believe in his politics, his vision for a better society.

But I’m struggling to defend his leadership. Not because I don’t believe in him, but because he seems to have stopped believing in himself. The fire that inspired millions has been dimmed by compromise and crisis management.

The Jeremy who stood up to Blair, who opposed the Iraq War, who never wavered in his principles - where is he now? Where’s the clarity, the conviction, the moral authority that made us follow him into battle?

The Wales Factor

Here in Wales, we’re facing a particular nightmare. Plaid Cymru are positioning themselves as the “real” remain party for Welsh speakers, the Lib Dems are targeting our English-speaking remain seats, and the Brexit Party could split our leave vote with the Tories.

Mark Drakeford’s doing his best in the Senedd, but he can’t save us from this mess. Welsh Labour’s reputation won’t survive a UK-wide catastrophe.

Preparing for Defeat

My comrades and I are still campaigning, still knocking doors, still trying to convince people. But increasingly, it feels like we’re preparing for defeat rather than fighting for victory.

We’re talking about saving what we can, holding onto our safest seats, minimizing the damage. It’s no way to go into an election, but it might be the only way to survive one.

The Movement Will Survive

Whatever happens in the coming election - and I fear the worst - the movement Jeremy built will survive. The hundreds of thousands who joined the party, who believed another world was possible, who tasted what real political hope feels like - they won’t just disappear.

But it might take a generation to rebuild from the rubble we’re about to create. And by then, how much damage will the Tories have done? How many more people will have died from austerity? How much further will inequality have grown?

A Final Thought

I’m writing this from my kitchen table in mid-Wales, looking out at hills that have seen centuries of struggle. Workers fighting for rights, communities defending their way of life, ordinary people standing up to power.

They didn’t give up when things looked hopeless, and neither will we. But Christ, it’s hard to keep faith when your own side seems determined to snatch defeat from the jaws of victory.

The election will come, probably within days. We’ll fight it as hard as we can with what we have. But I’m not optimistic.

Sometimes being right isn’t enough. Sometimes good people make bad decisions. Sometimes the movement you love breaks your heart.

From the valleys to the coast, solidarity forever. Even when it hurts.
